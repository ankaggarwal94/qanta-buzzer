# Poster Guidance Summary

These notes summarize the specific poster guidance relevant to redesigning the
attached poster.

## Google Doc guidelines

Source:
- `poster_guidelines/google_doc_poster_guidelines.txt`
- Original URL:
  - `https://docs.google.com/document/d/1PKK8bFriqfjrDhWYtZ0FWsVTjijjFVPpm9F2ELDYCuI/edit?tab=t.0`

Key points:
- The poster should give a quick summary of the work and motivate the viewer to
  learn more.
- Suggested sections:
  - Problem
  - Background
  - Methods
  - Experiments
  - Analysis
  - Conclusions
  - References
- Formatting suggestions:
  - use larger font sizes
  - prefer bullets over long paragraphs
  - use color to emphasize interesting findings
  - be visual, not textual
  - for results, a graph can often say more than a table
- Printing guidance:
  - landscape orientation is recommended
  - `20" x 30"` is recommended because foam is provided in that size
  - other sizes may be acceptable, but should not be too big or too small

## Ed thread guidance

Sources:
- `ed_threads.md`
- `ed_threads.json`

Relevant guidance extracted from those files:
- The poster should ideally be a concise version of the report.
- It should be a roughly `20x30` small poster.
- Main sections should include intro/problem, methods, results, conclusion, and
  related essentials.
- Figures and important math equations are encouraged.
- A template was suggested in a Stanford ICME poster link.

## Practical implications for the redesign

- Keep the poster landscape and approximately `30 x 20` inches.
- Favor a visual hierarchy with a few strong figures over dense prose.
- Use bigger type and fewer total words.
- Keep only a small number of references.
- Use actual metrics from `evaluation_report.json`; do not invent results.
- Make smoke results look like a labeled sanity-check snapshot rather than a
  polished final benchmark claim.
