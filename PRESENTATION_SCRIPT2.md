**Script:**

We're presenting our CS234 final project: a reinforcement-learning buzzer for multiple-choice pyramidal quiz bowl. The core question is *when* should an agent stop listening to clues and commit to an answer.

---

## Frame 2 — Problem

**Visual:** Bullet list on a white card.

**Script:**

Quiz bowl questions reveal evidence incrementally, clue by clue. A good system must decide **when** to buzz, not just *what* answer to pick. Buzz too early and you risk being wrong. Buzz too late and you lose strategic value. We study this in a multiple-choice setting so the answer space is controlled and evaluation is reproducible.

---

## Frame 3 — Background And Setup

**Visual:** Two-column layout. Left: "Sequential decision problem" with bullets. Right: "Two policy families" — belief-feature buzzers and T5 text-policy buzzers.

**Script:**

We model quiz bowl as a sequential decision problem over partial clues. Each example has question prefixes over time and K=4 answer options — one correct plus three distractors. We support two policy families: belief-feature buzzers (threshold, softmax-profile, Bayesian, PPO on structured obs) and end-to-end T5 text-policy buzzers (supervised + PPO).

---

## Frame 4 — Why Multiple Choice?

**Visual:** Left top card: advantages. Left bottom: challenges. Right card: design goals.

**Script:**

Open-ended answering adds aliasing and grading complexity. Multiple choice lets us isolate the buzzing decision itself. But naive distractors create artifacts — a model can exploit surface-level answer quirks. So our design goals are: keep the answer space constrained, and make the options hard enough that the agent must actually use the clues. We apply anti-artifact guards: alias collision checks, token overlap, length ratio, and question-text overlap.

---

## Frame 5 — Method Overview

**Visual:** Seven numbered steps in a grid layout showing the end-to-end pipeline.

**Script:**

The full pipeline: (1) Load tossup questions, (2) Build answer profiles from historical text, (3) Construct artifact-resistant MC questions, (4) Score options with a likelihood model (TF-IDF, SBERT, T5, or OpenAI embeddings), (5) Convert beliefs into observations, (6) Run baseline or learned buzzers, (7) Evaluate timing, correctness, calibration, and controls.

---

## Frame 6 — Our Contribution

**Visual:** Bold thesis statement at top, bullet list of contributions below.

**Script:**

We study pyramidal quiz bowl under a restricted multiple-choice action space and apply PPO to learn when to wait versus buzz. To our knowledge, this combination has not been explored in prior work. This lets us directly evaluate whether learned policies rely on sequentially revealed clues or on generic answer-choice patterns. We also contribute anti-artifact MC construction and calibration-focused evaluation beyond raw accuracy.

---

## Frame 7 — Results

**Visual:** Data table with columns: Metric, Supervised baseline, PPO (50 iterations), Direction. Orange banner at bottom.

**Script:**

The supervised baseline achieves 68% overall accuracy, dropping to 41% on early clues and reaching 79% on late clues. After 50 PPO iterations, average reward increased 35% from +0.31 to +0.42, mean buzz position shifted from 2.3 to 3.1 clues (more cautious), and expected calibration error improved from 0.18 to 0.15. The agent trades accuracy for more cautious buzzing while calibration improves.

---

## Frame 8 — Why Is PPO Accuracy Lower Than Supervised?

**Visual:** Two-column layout. Left: "Not a bug — it's the intended trade-off" with orange bullets. Right: "What PPO gains instead" with green bullets.

**Script:**

PPO's overall accuracy (64%) is lower than supervised (68%) — this is intentional. The supervised model is forced to guess at the end of every question. PPO learns to WAIT when uncertain, which means some questions time out unanswered, reducing raw accuracy. But PPO gains: reward up 35%, more cautious buzzing (2.3→3.1 clues), better calibration (ECE 0.18→0.15), and fewer confident-but-wrong answers. PPO optimizes expected reward, not raw accuracy — lower accuracy + better timing = higher overall value.

## Frame 9 — MC Stopping Intuition

**Visual:** Two side-by-side cards. Left: "Pyramidal tossup" with bullet points about clue difficulty. Right: "Multiple-choice twist" showing a 2×2 answer grid (A/B/C/D).

**Script:**

Now let's build the intuition for the optimal stopping formulation. Quiz bowl is pyramidal: early clues are obscure, later clues are easy. The answer space is finite and fixed from the start. The RL question at each prefix: should the policy WAIT or BUZZ with one of the answers?

## Frame 22 — Section Header: Abstain Semantics

**Visual:** Red section card. "Section 3: abstain means continue, not NOTA."

**Script:**

A crucial semantic point. When the model *abstains*, it does **not** mean "none of the above."

---

## Frames 23–25 — Abstain Stages 0–2

**Visual:** Answer grid + posterior bars + "ABSTAIN" label + red X over "NOTA".

- **Stage 0 (Frame 23):** Gold answer is always in the option set. Abstain = "I'd rather hear one more clue than commit now." It's a continuation action, not a rejection.
- **Stage 1 (Frame 24):** Even when A is at 52%, waiting can still be rational — another clue has positive value of information.
- **Stage 2 (Frame 25):** Abstain is about continuation value, not exhaustivity of the answer set.

---

## Frame 26 — Section Header: Factorization

**Visual:** Purple section card. "Section 4: factor answer quality and stop timing."

**Script:**

Instead of training one monolithic policy over 5 actions, we can factor the decision into two modules.

---

## Frames 27–28 — Factorization Stages 0–1

**Visual:** Flow diagram: prefix → answer module (p_ans) + stop module (p_buzz) → flat interface.

- **Stage 0 (Frame 27):** Answer module outputs distribution over answers — p_ans(i | h_t).
- **Stage 1 (Frame 28):** Stop module outputs scalar p_buzz. P(WAIT) = 1 − p_buzz, P(BUZZ i) = p_buzz × p_ans(i). Same semantics, cleaner implementation.

## Frame 30 — Training Recipe

**Visual:** Three colored cards: (1) answer model first, (2) hazard-loss warm start, (3) PPO fine-tuning.

**Script:**

Three-stage pipeline: first train the answer distribution with supervised learning, then warm-start the stop module with a hazard loss, then let PPO optimize the full task reward. RL focuses on the stopping decision because the answer module is already good.

## Frame 37 — Evaluation

**Visual:** Two-column layout. Left: "Metrics" (S_q, buzz accuracy, mean buzz step, ECE, Brier, per-category). Right: "Controls" (choices-only, shuffle, alias substitution, distractor difficulty).

**Script:**
