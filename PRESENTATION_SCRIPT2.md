## Frame 2 — Problem

**Visual:** Bullet list on a white card.

**Script:**

Quiz bowl questions reveal evidence incrementally, clue by clue. A good system must decide **when** to buzz, not just *what* answer to pick. Buzz too early and you risk being wrong. Buzz too late and you lose strategic value. We study this in a multiple-choice setting so the answer space is controlled and evaluation is reproducible.

## Frame 4 — Why Multiple Choice?

**Visual:** Left top card: "Advantages" in green (eliminates aliasing complexity; isolates the buzzing decision; controlled evaluation). Left bottom card: "Challenge" in red (naive distractors create artifacts; answer generation quality matters). Right card with blue accent: "Design goals" (constrained answer space; options hard enough to require clues; anti-artifact guards: alias collision, token overlap, length ratio, question-text overlap).

**Script:**

Open-ended answering adds aliasing and grading complexity. Multiple choice lets us isolate the buzzing decision itself. But naive distractors create artifacts — a model can exploit surface-level answer quirks. So our design goals are: keep the answer space constrained, and make the options hard enough that the agent must actually use the clues. We apply anti-artifact guards: alias collision checks, token overlap, length ratio, and question-text overlap.

## Frame 8 — How To Read PPO Metrics

**Visual:** Two-column layout. Left: "Why raw accuracy can dip" with orange bullets (PPO learns timing, not just final guess; waiting avoids low-confidence answers; accuracy alone is not the right summary statistic). Orange callout box with actual smoke-run accuracy comparison. Right: "What to compare alongside it" with green bullets (mean S_q; mean buzz step; calibration; controls for clue use vs artifact use; strategic abstention). Green callout: "A PPO buzzer is only better if its timing behavior improves the metrics you actually care about."

**Script:**

PPO's overall accuracy may be lower than the best baseline — this is intentional. The supervised baseline is forced to guess at the end of every question. PPO learns to WAIT when uncertain, which means some questions time out unanswered, reducing raw accuracy. But PPO gains: better task reward, more cautious buzzing, improved calibration, and fewer confident-but-wrong answers. PPO optimizes expected reward, not raw accuracy — lower accuracy + better timing = higher overall value. Always compare alongside S_q, timing profile, and calibration.

## Frame 28 — Factorization Stage 1

**Visual:** Same flow diagram, now fully colored. Answer module (blue). Stop module (orange accent): "outputs p_buzz(h_t)". Right panel (purple accent): "Flat action interface" — P(WAIT) = 1 − p_buzz, P(BUZZ i) = p_buzz × p_ans(i). Bottom callout.

**Script:**

The stop module outputs a scalar p_buzz. P(WAIT) = 1 − p_buzz, P(BUZZ i) = p_buzz × p_ans(i). Same semantics as the flat 5-action space, but cleaner implementation: factor answer choice and stop timing internally, then flatten only if the RL stack needs it.
