## Frame 2 — Problem

<details><summary>🖼 Visual</summary>
Bullet list on a white card.
</details>

> **The motivation is twofold. Existing QA benchmarks only measure final accuracy, ignoring uncertainty and confidence calibration — a model that guesses confidently but wrong is scored the same as one that knows it's uncertain. Quiz bowl's incremental clue structure exposes a timing problem that standard benchmarks miss: information arrives over time, so a good agent must decide WHEN to commit, not just WHAT to answer. This makes it a natural testbed for studying sequential decision-making under partial observability, where the trade-off between waiting for more evidence and answering early is the core challenge.**

> **We model quiz bowl as a sequential decision problem over partial clues. Each example has question prefixes over time and K equals 4 answer options — one correct plus three distractors. We support two policy families: belief-feature buzzers (threshold, softmax-profile, Bayesian, PPO on structured obs) and end-to-end T5 text-policy buzzers (supervised plus PPO).**

---

## Frame 4 — Why Multiple Choice?

<details><summary>🖼 Visual</summary>
Left top: "Advantages" in green. Left bottom: "Challenge" in red. Right: "Design goals" with blue accent.
</details>

> **Open-ended answering adds aliasing and grading complexity. Multiple choice lets us isolate the buzzing decision itself. But naive distractors create artifacts — a model can exploit surface-level answer quirks. So our design goals are: keep the answer space constrained, and make the options hard enough that the agent must actually use the clues. We apply anti-artifact guards: alias collision checks, token overlap, length ratio, and question-text overlap.**

<details><summary>🖼 Visual</summary>

</details>

> **The full pipeline: one, load tossup questions. Two, build answer profiles from historical text. Three, construct artifact-resistant MC questions. Four, score options with a likelihood model — TF-IDF, SBERT, T5, or OpenAI embeddings. Five, convert beliefs into observations. Six, run buzzer agents. Seven, evaluate timing, correctness, calibration, and controls.**

## Frame 8 — How To Read PPO Metrics

<details><summary>🖼 Visual</summary>
Two-column layout. Left: "Why raw accuracy can dip" (orange). Right: "What to compare alongside it" (green).
</details>

> **PPO's overall accuracy may be lower than the best baseline — this is intentional. The supervised baseline is forced to guess at the end of every question. PPO learns to WAIT when uncertain, which means some questions time out unanswered, reducing raw accuracy. But PPO gains: better task reward, more cautious buzzing, improved calibration, and fewer confident-but-wrong answers. PPO optimizes expected reward, not raw accuracy — lower accuracy plus better timing equals higher overall value.**

## Frame 28 — Factorization Stage 1

<details><summary>🖼 Visual</summary>
Full flow diagram colored. Flat action interface (purple): P(WAIT) = 1 − p_buzz, P(BUZZ i) = p_buzz × p_ans(i).
</details>

> **The stop module outputs a scalar p_buzz. P of WAIT equals 1 minus p_buzz. P of BUZZ i equals p_buzz times p_ans of i. Same semantics as the flat 5-action space, but cleaner implementation: factor answer choice and stop timing internally, then flatten only if the RL stack needs it.**
