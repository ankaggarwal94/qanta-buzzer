### Running example

All posterior, value-chart, abstain, and factorization frames use a single **Markov tossup** from QBReader (2022 ARCADIA, packet 08, tossup 11). The question repeatedly uses the anaphoric phrase "named for this person" / "a process named for this person" until the giveaway clue "memoryless chain." The four answer options are:

| Label | Answer |
|-------|--------|
| **A** | Andrey Andreyevich Markov |
| B | Leonhard Euler |
| C | Carl Friedrich Gauss |
| D | Augustin-Louis Cauchy |

Option A is always gold. The distractors are a cleaner mathematician-only set drawn from answers that already exist in the local corpus.

<div class="script">We're presenting our CS234 final project: a reinforcement-learning buzzer for multiple-choice pyramidal quiz bowl. The core question is WHEN should an agent stop listening to clues and commit to an answer.</div>

<hr class="section-break">

<div class="frame-title">Frame 2 — Problem</div>

<div class="visual">Bullet list on a white card.</div>

<div class="script">The motivation is twofold. Existing QA benchmarks only measure final accuracy, ignoring uncertainty and confidence calibration — a model that guesses confidently but wrong is scored the same as one that knows it's uncertain. Quiz bowl's incremental clue structure exposes a timing problem that standard benchmarks miss: information arrives over time, so a good agent must decide WHEN to commit, not just WHAT to answer. This makes it a natural testbed for studying sequential decision-making under partial observability, where the trade-off between waiting for more evidence and answering early is the core challenge.</div>

<hr class="section-break">

<div class="frame-title">Frame 3 — Background And Setup</div>

<div class="visual">Two-column layout. Left: "Sequential decision problem" with bullets. Right: "Two policy families" — belief-feature buzzers and T5 text-policy buzzers.</div>

<div class="script">We model quiz bowl as a sequential decision problem over partial clues. Each example has question prefixes over time and K equals 4 answer options — one correct plus three distractors. We support two policy families: belief-feature buzzers (threshold, softmax-profile, Bayesian, PPO on structured obs) and end-to-end T5 text-policy buzzers (supervised plus PPO).</div>

<hr class="section-break">

<div class="frame-title">Frame 4 — Why Multiple Choice?</div>

<div class="visual">Left top: "Advantages" in green. Left bottom: "Challenge" in red. Right: "Design goals" with blue accent.</div>

<div class="script">Open-ended answering adds aliasing and grading complexity. Multiple choice lets us isolate the buzzing decision itself. But naive distractors create artifacts — a model can exploit surface-level answer quirks. So our design goals are: keep the answer space constrained, and make the options hard enough that the agent must actually use the clues. We apply anti-artifact guards: alias collision checks, token overlap, length ratio, and question-text overlap.</div>

<hr class="section-break">

<div class="frame-title">Frame 5 — Method Overview</div>

<div class="visual">Seven numbered steps in a two-column grid layout, each as a colored pill.</div>

<div class="script">The full pipeline: one, load tossup questions. Two, build answer profiles from historical text. Three, construct artifact-resistant MC questions. Four, score options with a likelihood model — TF-IDF, SBERT, T5, or OpenAI embeddings. Five, convert beliefs into observations. Six, run buzzer agents. Seven, evaluate timing, correctness, calibration, and controls.</div>

<hr class="section-break">

<div class="frame-title">Frame 6 — Our Contribution</div>

<div class="visual">Bold thesis statement at top, purple bullet list of five contributions.</div>

<div class="script">We study pyramidal quiz bowl under a restricted multiple-choice action space and apply PPO to learn when to wait versus buzz. To our knowledge, this combination has not been explored in prior work. This lets us directly evaluate whether learned policies rely on sequentially revealed clues or on generic answer-choice patterns. We also contribute anti-artifact MC construction and calibration-focused evaluation beyond raw accuracy.</div>

<div class="frame-title">Frame 8 — How To Read PPO Metrics</div>

<div class="visual">Two-column layout. Left: "Why raw accuracy can dip" (orange). Right: "What to compare alongside it" (green).</div>

<div class="script">PPO's overall accuracy may be lower than the best baseline — this is intentional. The supervised baseline is forced to guess at the end of every question. PPO learns to WAIT when uncertain, which means some questions time out unanswered, reducing raw accuracy. But PPO gains: better task reward, more cautious buzzing, improved calibration, and fewer confident-but-wrong answers. PPO optimizes expected reward, not raw accuracy — lower accuracy plus better timing equals higher overall value.</div>

<div class="frame-title">Frame 26 — § Factor Stop And Answer</div>

<div class="visual">Section header card with purple accent.</div>

<div class="script">Instead of training one monolithic policy over 5 actions, we can factor the decision into two modules.</div>

<hr class="section-break">

<div class="frame-title">Frame 27 — Factorization Stage 0</div>

<div class="visual">Flow diagram. Answer module (blue): outputs p_ans. Stop module: outputs p_buzz.</div>

<div class="script">The answer module outputs a distribution over answers: p_ans of i given h_t. This is the "what to answer" part, trained first via supervised learning.</div>

<hr class="section-break">

<div class="frame-title">Frame 28 — Factorization Stage 1</div>

<div class="visual">Full flow diagram colored. Flat action interface (purple): P(WAIT) = 1 − p_buzz, P(BUZZ i) = p_buzz × p_ans(i).</div>

<div class="script">The stop module outputs a scalar p_buzz. P of WAIT equals 1 minus p_buzz. P of BUZZ i equals p_buzz times p_ans of i. Same semantics as the flat 5-action space, but cleaner implementation: factor answer choice and stop timing internally, then flatten only if the RL stack needs it.</div>
